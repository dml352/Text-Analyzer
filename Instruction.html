Background:

  1. In his 1948 article "A New Readability Yardstick" in the Journal of Applied Psychology, Rudolf Flesch proposed a readability index to determine how difficult a piece of text is to read. The text is analyzed to generate an index (usually) between 0 and 100. The lower the number, the harder the text is to read. Here are some Flesch readability indices for various publications:

    Comics 95
    Consumer Ads 82
    Sports Illustrated 65
    Time 57
    New York Times 39
    Auto Insurance Policy 10
    Internal Revenue Code -6
    The Flesch Reading Ease Score (FRES) is computed using the following formula.

    FRES = 206.835 - 84.6 * (Number of Syllables) / (Number of Words) - 1.015 * (Number of Words) / (Number of Sentences)

    The index can also be converted to educational levels using the following chart:

    91-100 Fifth Grade Level
    81-90 Sixth Grade Level
    71-80 Seventh Grade Level
    66-70 Eighth Grade Level
    61-65 Ninth Grade Level
    51-60

    High School Student
    31-50 College Student
    0-30 College Graduate
    Less than 0 Law School Graduate
  2. The Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level Formula provides a direct calculation to determine grade level. The Flesch-Kincaid Reading Age (FKRA) is given by

    FKRA = 11.8* (Number of Syllables) / (Number of Words) + 0.39 * (Number of Words) / (Number of Sentences) - 15.59

    The result is a number which corresponds roughly to a student's grade level, e.g., a score of 11.3 would be appropriate for an 11th grade student, and scores of 13 or higher correspond to students in higher education levels.

  3. In 1969, G Harry McLaughlin created the SMOG (Simple Measure Of Gobbledygook) Readability Formula, which similarly assesses text readability. As the process was meant to be done by humans, some of the details require decision-making that is not easily done with a program. For our purposes, we will use a simplified SMOG algorithm:

    • define a polySyllable word as a word with 3 or more syllables.
    • determine the density of polySyllable words in the text by dividing the number of polySyllable words by the number of sentences.
    • multiply the density by 30, to produce an indicator
    • determine the square root of the number that is the closest perfect square to the indicator. That number may be lower or higher than the indicator. For instance, 19 is closer to 16 than to 25, so if the indicator is 19, the result would be 4, but if the indicator is 21, the result would be 5.
    • add 3 to the result to produce the SMOG grade level estimate.

Notes:

  • When counting words in the file, a "word" is is a sequence of letters, minus punctuation, determined by white space or the end of a line, regardless of whether or not it is an actual English Word. For instance, consider the sentence
    "The Market-Frankford Line's the fastest way," she said.
    This sentence contains 8 words, which are The, MarketFrankford, Lines, the, fastest, way, she, said - notice that two of the words have internal punctuation marks, and so those should not be construed as word endings.
  • To estimate the number of syllables in each word, use the number of "independent vowels" as a guide. Any group of adjacent vowels (a, e, i, o, u, y) will count as one "independent vowel". For example the ea in real counts as one syllable. An e at the end of a word does not count as a syllable. A word with no vowels still counts as having one syllable.
  • Each period, colon, semicolon, colon, question mark, or exclamation point indicates the end of a sentence.
  • Note: these estimates will not be perfect. For example, "Mrs. Peterson bought leopard-skin leotards." is one sentence, but would probably be counted as two sentences. Also,"leopard" has two syllables while "leotard" has three syllables, but both would be estimated as two syllables with these rules.
